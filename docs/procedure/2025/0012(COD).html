Additional customs duties on imports of certain products originating in the USA
Subjects: Bilateral economic and trade agreements and relations; Union Customs Code, tariffs, preferential arrangements, rules of origin
Stage reached: Procedure completed
Reference: 2025/0012(COD)

Commission's Proposal Summary

The European Commission has proposed a legislative amendment aimed at refining the process of imposing additional customs duties on certain imports from the United States. This proposal is a response to the U.S.'s Byrd Amendment, which was found to violate World Trade Organization (WTO) rules. The amendment introduces a "de minimis" threshold of USD 30,000 for the disbursement of duties collected from the EU. If disbursements fall below this threshold, no additional duties will be imposed, which is intended to reduce administrative burdens and costs for the EU. This change aligns with EU trade policies by maintaining compliance with WTO obligations while promoting efficient resource use. The proposal is part of the EU's common commercial policy, an area of exclusive competence for the Union. It is considered proportionate as it avoids unnecessary administrative costs when retaliation is negligible. Historically, the U.S. was authorized to suspend tariff concessions under the WTO due to non-compliance with the Byrd Amendment, leading to a 4.3% ad valorem additional customs duty on certain U.S. imports. However, recent data shows a negligible level of nullification or impairment, resulting in a 0% additional import duty to avoid disproportionate administrative costs. Given the effective repeal of the Byrd Amendment in 2007 and the continued low level of nullification or impairment, the proposal suggests amending existing regulations to include a de minimis threshold. Financially, the proposal is deemed to have no significant impact.
