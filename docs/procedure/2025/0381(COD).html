Banks and financial markets: settlement finality in payment and securities settlement systems
Subjects: Securities and financial markets, stock exchange, CIUTS, investments; Electronic money and payments, cross-border credit transfers; Financial supervision
Stage reached: Preparatory phase in Parliament
Reference: 2025/0381(COD)

Commission's Proposal Summary

The European Commission has introduced a new regulation aimed at enhancing the integration and efficiency of Europe's financial markets by focusing on the finality of settlements in payment and securities systems. This proposal seeks to replace the existing Settlement Finality Directive, ensuring that once transactions are entered into a designated system, they cannot be reversed, even if a participant becomes insolvent. The primary goals are to provide legal certainty, reduce systemic risks, and facilitate smoother financial operations across the EU. This initiative is part of a broader effort to create a Savings and Investments Union, making Europe a more attractive destination for investment by addressing the need for risk capital in strategic sectors. The proposal aims to eliminate legal uncertainties and reduce costs associated with cross-border financial transactions, fostering a more integrated and resilient European capital market. It seeks to enhance liquidity, spur innovation, and provide better financial products and services to EU citizens. The existing Settlement Finality Directive has faced challenges due to its national transposition, resulting in significant divergences that undermine its effectiveness in fostering a coherent and integrated European post-trade landscape. Member States have adopted different approaches to the designation of systems, creating inconsistencies in the scope of protection available to participants, with varying definitions and treatment of participants and indirect participants. To address these issues, the proposal supports the correct and safe functioning of the single market, safeguarding competition and preserving financial stability. It introduces a common definition and harmonized approach to system designation, with the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) playing a key role.
