What's the European Union?

The European Union (EU) is a unique political and economic partnership made up of 27 European countries. With more than 450 million citizens, it combines features of both a federation and a confederation, allowing its member states to cooperate closely while remaining sovereign nations.


System of Shared Sovereignty



Everything the EU does is based on treaties that are voluntarily signed and ratified by all member states. The two main legal foundations of the EU are the Treaty on European Union (TEU) and the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU). Through these treaties, countries agree to transfer certain powers to the EU so that decisions can be taken collectively when this is considered more effective.


The EU Institutions and How They Work Together



EU laws are mainly shaped by three institutions: European Commission, the European Parliament, and the Council of the European Union. The European Commission proposes new laws and ensures that EU rules are properly applied. The European Parliament, which represents EU citizens, and the Council of the European Union, which represents national governments, jointly examine, amend, and vote on these proposals. This process is known as the ordinary legislative procedure. For a law to be adopted, both the Parliament and the Council must agree on the final text. In the Council, most votes are taken using qualified majority voting, which means that decisions require the support of a majority of member states representing a majority of the EU population.

The people who take decisions at the EU level are either directly or indirectly elected. Members of the European Parliament (MEPs) are directly elected by EU citizens every five years in European elections. National governments, which form the Council of the European Union, are elected at the national level by their own citizens. The President of the European Commission is proposed by the European Council—composed of elected national leaders—and must be approved by the European Parliament. This system ensures that EU decision-making is linked, in different ways, to democratic elections across the member states


How European Parliament Elections Work



European Parliament elections take place once every five years and allow EU citizens to choose their representatives at the European level. The most recent elections were held from 6–9 June 2024, and the next will be in 2029.

All eligible EU citizens have the right to vote directly for Members of the European Parliament (MEPs) in these elections. Every voter has a single vote and an equal say in choosing who represents them in the Parliament. To ensure that the Parliament reflects voters’ political preferences fairly, the elections use a proportional representation system in every EU member state. This means that the number of seats a political party wins is roughly proportional to the share of votes it receives in that country. For example, if a party gets 30% of the vote in a country that elects 10 MEPs, they’ll get about 3 seats.

Under these rules, every vote counts the same within each member state in terms of contributing to how many seats a party wins. Because countries vary in size, the number of seats per country is determined by population (with a minimum of 6 and a maximum of 96 seats per state), but within each state the proportional system ensures equal treatment of each vote for seat allocation.

Once votes are counted, seats are distributed to parties according to their share of votes. In countries with a threshold (such as 5% in some places like France), parties must pass that minimum share to get seats; other countries may have different rules but always proportionally.


How Decisions Are Made



EU decision-making is guided by the two key principles. The principle of conferral means that the EU can only act in areas where the member states have explicitly given it authority. On the other hand, subsidiarity means that the EU should only take action when objectives cannot be sufficiently achieved by individual countries and would be better handled at the European level.

The EU has exclusive competence in certain areas, such as monetary policy for countries using the euro and common trade policy. In many other fields—such as policing, justice, education, and labor law—member states keep primary responsibility.


How EU Laws Take Effect



Once a decision is adopted, the EU can use different legal instruments, notably regulations and directives. Regulations are directly applicable and become law in all member states immediately. Directives set binding objectives, but each country is free to decide how to achieve them through its national legislation.
